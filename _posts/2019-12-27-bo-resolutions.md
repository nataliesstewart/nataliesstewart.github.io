Mahowald's got this really interesting paper, called
[$$\bo$$-resolutions](https://projecteuclid.org/euclid.pjm/1102736799), where he
describes the $$\bo$$-based Adams spectral sequence, and uses it to prove the
telescope conjecture at height $$1$$. He derives differentials in this spectral
sequence by comparison to the $$A$$-based Adams spectral sequence, where $$A =
\S\mmod\nu$$ (see Section 2 [here](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.10534) --- that
paper contains the details of some of what I did this summer!). Mark and a bunch
of other people have a paper on
[$$\tmf$$-resolutions](https://www3.nd.edu/~mbehren1/papers/tmfASSZ.pdf), and
I'm working on a project that uses Mahowald's techniques to obtain differentials
in the $$\tmf$$-ASS via this spectrum $$B$$ that I study in my Ando-Hopkins-Rezk
surjectivity paper. Before I talk about that, I want to discuss Mahowald's
paper. It's incredibly dense, and consequently is virtually impenetrable by a
mortal like me. I'd like to record what (little) I understand of it.

There are a few results we'll take for granted/not discuss here; see also
Section 2 of [BBBCX](https://www3.nd.edu/~mbehren1/papers/boASS6.pdf), whose
notation we'll be following. One of these results is the following: let
$$\H\Z_i$$ denote the $$i$$th integral Brown-Gitler spectrum.  Then

$$\bo \wedge \bo \simeq \bigoplus_{k\geq 0} \bo \wedge \Sigma^{4k} \H\Z_k.$$

(Later, I'll talk about how this relates to the splitting of the Tate spectrum
$$\bo^{tC_2}$$.) One can also determine the homotopy type of $$\bo \wedge
\H\Z_k$$: Mahowald shows that

$$\bo \wedge \H\Z_k = \bo^{\langle 2k - \alpha(k)\rangle} \vee \text{mod 2
Eilenberg-Maclane spectra},$$

where $$\alpha(k)$$ is the sum of digits in the base $$2$$-expansion of $$k$$,
and $$\bo^{\langle i\rangle}$$ denotes the $$i$$th Adams cover of $$\bo$$ (so
$$\{ \bo^{\langle \bullet\rangle} \}$$ forms a minimal Adams resolution of
$$\bo$$).

The $$\bo$$-Adams spectral sequence runs

$$^{\bo} E_1^{s,t} = \pi_{t-s} \bo \wedge \ol{\bo}^{\wedge s} \Rightarrow \pi_t
\S_{(2)}.$$

Iterating the identification of $$\bo \wedge \bo$$ splits up the $$E_1$$-term,
and in particular, the mod $$2$$ Eilenbeg-Maclane summands give rise to a
subcomplex $$V^{\ast, \ast}$$ of the $$E_1$$-page. The BBBCX paper calls this
the "evil" complex. The quotient of the $$E_1$$-page by $$V$$ gives rise to
the "good" complex $$\cC^{\ast, \ast}$$.

If $$I = (i_1, \cdots, i_n)$$, then let $$|\!|I|\!| = \sum_j i_j$$, and let
$$\alpha(I) = \sum_j \alpha(i_j)$$. Then 

$$\cC^{n,\ast} = \bigoplus_{|I| = n} \Sigma^{4|\!|I|\!|} \pi_\ast b_I,$$

where

$$b_I = \begin{cases}
\bo^{\langle 2|\!|I|\!| - \alpha(I)\rangle} & |\!|I|\!|\text{ even}\\
\bsp^{\langle 2|\!|I|\!| - \alpha(I) - 1\rangle} & |\!|I|\!|\text{ odd}.
\end{cases}$$

Let's get to the differentials. Write $$x(I)$$ for an element $$x\in \pi_\ast
b_I$$. Generators of $$\cC^{\ast,\ast}$$ are of the form $$2^{k-2m} v_1^{2m}
\eta^a$$, where $$0\leq a \leq 2$$. Then (see Theorem 3.7
[here](https://www.ams.org/journals/tran/1987-300-02/S0002-9947-1987-0876468-1/S0002-9947-1987-0876468-1.pdf)):

$$d_1(2^{k-2m} v_1^{2m}(i_1, \cdots, i_n)) = \sum_{m' + m'' = m} 2^{k - 2m'}
\binom{m}{m'} v_1^{2m'} (m'', i_1, \cdots, i_n) + \sum_{1\leq j \leq n}
\sum_{i_j' + i_j'' = i_j} 2^{k-2m} \binom{i_j}{i_j'} v_1^{2m}(i_1, \cdots, i_j',
i_j'', \cdots, i_n),$$

and everything else is determined by $$\eta$$-linearity.

How is this differential determined? Well, the first sum simply comes from the
expression for the right unit on $$2^? v_1^{2?}$$ in $$(\BPP_\ast, \BPP_\ast
\BPP)$$. The other part of this differential is determined by comparison to the
$$A$$-based ASS.  Recall that $$A = \S \mmod \nu$$ is the $$\E_{1}$$-quotient of
$$\S$$ by $$\nu$$. There is an $$\E_{1}$$-map $$A\to \bo$$ (again, see [my
paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.10534), or practically any of Mahowald's
papers from the '70s), which gives a map from the $$A$$-based ASS to the
$$\bo$$-based ASS. The idea is to transport differentials in the $$A$$-based ASS
to the $$\bo$$-based ASS. For this, we need to first understand differentials in
the $$A$$-based ASS --- as it turns out, we'll only need to think about
$$d_1$$-differentials for our applications to the $$\bo$$-ASS.

The $$A$$-based resolution of the sphere goes

$$A\to A \wedge A \to A \wedge A \wedge A \to \cdots$$

The Thom isomorphism gives an equivalence $$A \wedge A \simeq A \wedge
\Sigma^\infty_+ \Omega S^5$$, and the James splitting further gives an
equivalence $$\Sigma^\infty_+ \Omega S^5 \simeq \bigoplus_{k\geq 0} \Sigma^{4k}
A$$. This is very closely related to the splitting of $$\bo \wedge \bo$$: there
is a diagram

$$\begin{array}{ccc}
A \wedge A & \to & \bigoplus_{k\geq 0} \Sigma^{4k} A \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow\\
\bo \wedge \bo & \to & \bigoplus_{k\geq 0} \Sigma^{4k} \bo \wedge \H\Z_k,
\end{array} \quad (\ast)$$

which commutes up to elements of Adams filtration $$\geq 2$$ (not sure I fully
understand why yet).

The differential in the $$A$$-ASS is determined by the map $$A\to A \wedge A$$,
which in turn is determined by the maps $$A\to \Sigma^{4k} A$$ coming from the
James splitting. Since the map $$A\to A \wedge A$$ is obtained by Thomification
of the map

$$\Omega S^5 \to \Omega S^5 \times \Omega S^5 \xrightarrow{\Delta \times 1}
\Omega S^5\times \Omega S^5\times \Omega S^5\to \Omega S^5\times \Omega S^5,$$

it sends

$$\H_{4i}(\Omega S^5)\ni x_i\mapsto \sum_{0\leq j\leq i} \binom{i}{j} x_j
\otimes x_{i-j}\in \H_{4i}(\Omega S^5\times \Omega S^5).$$

(Everything's in mod $$2$$ homology.) Consequently, iterating this gives the
effect of the map $$A^{\wedge s}\to A^{\wedge s+1}$$ in homology. This tells us
the effect of $$d_1$$ on an ASS representative of an element of $${}^A
E_1^{\ast, \ast}$$.

In fact, $$\BPP_\ast(A) \cong \pi_\ast \BPP[x_4]$$, where $$x_4$$ is sent to
$$t_1^2\in \BPP_\ast(\BPP)$$ (see Proposition 2.6
[here](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.10534)). In fact, one can write $$\BPP_\ast(A)
\cong \BPP_\ast[t_1^2 + v_1 t_1]$$. Write

$$\tau = t_1^2 + v_1 t_1 = \frac{1}{4}(\eta_R(v_1^2) - v_1^2) = \frac{1}{4}((v_1
+ pt_1)^2 - v_1^2);$$

then $$\tau$$ is primitive in $$(\BPP_\ast, \BPP_\ast \BPP)$$, and detects
$$\nu$$ in the ANSS of the sphere. The calculation above of the coproduct in the
$$\BPP$$-Hopf algebroid for $$A$$ amounts to the expression for $$[\psi(\tau)]$$
on the top of page 9 [here](https://www3.nd.edu/~mbehren1/papers/boASS6.pdf).
Our entire argument above is actually just the BBBCX argument, rephrased in
terms of $$A$$.

(Note that the above formula for $$d_1$$ tells us that the map $$A\to
\Sigma^{4k} A$$ is of degree $$\binom{i}{k}$$ on the cell in dimension $$4i$$.
For instance, this implies that the map $$A\to \Sigma^4 A$$ is of degree $$k$$
in dimension $$4k$$.  Since there is a commutative (up to elements of Adams
filtration $$\geq 2$$) diagram

$$\begin{array}{ccc}
A & \to & \Sigma^{4} A \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow\\
\bo & \to & \Sigma^{4} \bo \wedge \H\Z_1,
\end{array}$$

we find that the map $$\bo \to \Sigma^4 \H\Z_1 \wedge \bo$$ is of degree $$k$$
in dimension $$4k$$. Consequently, this map increases Adams filtration by
$$\lfloor \log_2(k)\rfloor$$ in dimension $$\pi_{4k}$$. This is how you get
what's claimed right before Proposition 5.15 in [Mahowald's
paper](https://projecteuclid.org/euclid.pjm/1102736799).)

Once you've got these differentials, you need to calculate the cohomology of the
good complex. This turns out to be challenging, but doable (Corollary 4.9 of
BBBCX). I don't want to get into its calculation now. Importantly, there is a
slope $$1/5$$ vanishing line in the $$\bo$$-ASS: check out the BBBCX paper; they
explain it better than I can (in part because I don't fully grok the proof,
which is highly calculational).  One can use this to argue directly that the
cycles in $$E_2$$ in $$\bo$$-filtration $$0$$ and $$1$$ are permanent cycles;
see Section 8.4 of [this awesome
document](http://www.mit.edu/~sanathd/mahowald-unell-bott.pdf).
